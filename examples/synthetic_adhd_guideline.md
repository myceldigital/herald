# Management of Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder in Adults

**Synthetic Example Guideline Consortium (SEGC)**
Version 1.0 — March 2026

*This is a synthetic guideline created for demonstration purposes. It does not represent real clinical guidance from any healthcare organisation.*

## 1. Introduction

This guideline provides evidence-based recommendations for the diagnosis and management of Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD) in adults aged 18 years and over. It is intended for use by psychiatrists, general practitioners, nurse prescribers, and other healthcare professionals involved in ADHD care.

## 2. Diagnosis

Diagnosis of ADHD in adults should follow DSM-5 or ICD-11 criteria. A comprehensive assessment should include developmental history, current symptom profile, functional impairment assessment, and screening for comorbid conditions.

## 3. Pre-treatment assessment

### 3.1 Baseline assessments

Before initiating pharmacological treatment, the following baseline assessments should be completed: height and weight, blood pressure and heart rate, cardiovascular risk assessment, and baseline rating scales (ASRS, DIVA-5 or equivalent).

### 3.2 Cardiac screening

All patients with a personal or family history of cardiac disease should undergo ECG screening and specialist cardiac review before commencing ADHD pharmacotherapy. Stimulant and non-stimulant medications can affect heart rate, blood pressure, and cardiac rhythm.

## 4. Pharmacological treatment

### 4.1 First-line treatment

Methylphenidate is recommended as first-line pharmacological treatment for adults with ADHD. Extended-release formulations are preferred to reduce dosing complexity and diversion risk.

Initial dose: 18mg once daily in the morning. Titrate by 18mg increments at weekly intervals. Maximum dose: 72mg/day. Titration should be guided by clinical response and tolerability.

### 4.2 Comorbid conditions

#### 4.2.1 ADHD with anxiety

When ADHD presents with comorbid anxiety, clinicians should consider whether the anxiety is primary or secondary to ADHD-related functional impairment. Atomoxetine may address both conditions simultaneously. Alternatively, stabilise anxiety with an SSRI before introducing stimulant treatment.

If anxiety is mild and likely secondary to ADHD-related functional impairment, a trial of methylphenidate may be initiated with close monitoring of anxiety symptoms using GAD-7 at each review.

#### 4.2.2 ADHD with depression

Depression should generally be treated before or concurrently with ADHD when symptoms are moderate to severe. Untreated depression impairs the ability to assess true ADHD symptom burden and treatment response.

If depression is mild (PHQ-9 10-14), methylphenidate may be started first as improved ADHD function can secondarily improve mood. If depression is moderate-to-severe (PHQ-9 ≥ 15), initiate SSRI and reassess ADHD symptoms after 8 weeks.

#### 4.2.3 ADHD with substance use disorder

Non-stimulant medications should be considered first-line when substance use disorder is comorbid with ADHD. Atomoxetine carries no abuse liability. If stimulant treatment is required, lisdexamfetamine is preferred due to its prodrug pharmacokinetics reducing abuse potential.

Require substance use stability (minimum 3 months) before initiating stimulant treatment. Urine drug screening at baseline and periodically during treatment is recommended.

#### 4.2.5 ADHD with tic disorder

Current evidence does not support the historical view that stimulants are contraindicated in patients with tic disorders. However, individual patients may experience tic exacerbation and should be monitored closely during titration.

If tics worsen with stimulant initiation, consider switching to atomoxetine or guanfacine. Alpha-2 agonists (guanfacine, clonidine) may address both ADHD and tics.

## 5. Treatment failure pathways

### 5.1 Optimising current treatment

Before concluding that a stimulant trial has failed, clinicians should verify that the dose has been optimised and the trial duration has been adequate. Switching between stimulant classes (methylphenidate to amphetamine or vice versa) is recommended before moving to non-stimulant options.

Adequate trial: minimum 4 weeks at optimal dose (≥1mg/kg/day or maximum tolerated dose).

### 5.2 Cross-class stimulant switching

Cross-class stimulant switching is a well-established strategy. Network meta-analysis evidence suggests lisdexamfetamine may have a marginally larger effect size than methylphenidate in adults, though both are effective first-line agents.

Lisdexamfetamine: initial dose 30mg once daily. Titrate by 20mg increments at weekly intervals. Maximum dose 70mg/day. Approximately 40% of patients who do not respond to one stimulant class will respond to the other.

### 5.3 Non-stimulant options

Atomoxetine is the first-choice non-stimulant for adults with ADHD who have not responded to or cannot tolerate stimulant medications. Clinicians should be aware of the delayed onset of action (6-8 weeks) and counsel patients accordingly to prevent premature discontinuation.

Initial dose: 40mg once daily for 7 days, then 80mg once daily. Maximum 100mg/day if body weight exceeds 70kg. Allow 6-8 weeks for full therapeutic effect.

If atomoxetine is ineffective or not tolerated, consider guanfacine extended-release or bupropion as third-line options.

## 6. Special populations

### 6.1 Pregnancy

There is limited safety data for ADHD medications in pregnancy. The default recommendation is discontinuation with non-pharmacological support. Continuation may be considered in individual cases following specialist risk-benefit assessment.

If medication is continued, methylphenidate has the most pregnancy safety data. Document shared decision-making. Refer for specialist perinatal psychiatric review.

### 6.2 Breastfeeding

If ADHD medication is required during breastfeeding, methylphenidate is preferred due to its low relative infant dose. Infant monitoring is recommended regardless of the agent chosen.

Amphetamines transfer to breast milk and may cause infant irritability. Atomoxetine data is limited.

## 7. Non-pharmacological interventions

### 7.1 Psychosocial interventions

Non-pharmacological interventions should be offered to all adults with ADHD, either as standalone treatment for mild presentations or as an adjunct to medication for moderate-to-severe presentations.

Recommended interventions include structured psychoeducation, CBT adapted for ADHD (organisational skills, time management, emotional regulation), coaching, and workplace accommodations.

## 8. Monitoring

Blood pressure and heart rate should be measured at baseline, at each titration step, and every 6 months during stable treatment. Weight should be monitored every 3 months during the first year.

Rating scales (ASRS or equivalent) should be administered at baseline and at each clinical review to objectively measure treatment response.
